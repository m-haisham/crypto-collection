In computer science, password cracking is defined as the process of recovering passwords from data that has been stored in or transmitted by a computer system in scrambled form

Brute-Force Attack

A brute-force attack uses trial-and-error to guess the passwords or passphrases. It is the digital equivalent of trying every key in your key ring, and eventually finding the right one.

A brute-force attack can, in theory, be used to decrypt any encrypted key. However in practice this is not feasible. For longer passwords such an attack may take years to guess, as the length of the password increases the number possible combinations increase exponentially.

However for shorter password it is still a simple and reliable method to decrypt a password. And for longer passwords other methods like dictionary attack are used.

Dictionary Attack

A dictionary attack is a brute-force technique where the algorithm runs through a list of predefined words and phrases, such as those from a dictionary or previous data breaches.

Where traditional brute-force attacks may try every possible combination, a dictionary attack uses a limited number of pre-selected words. As a dictionary attack doesnt go through every possible combination it reduces the likelihood that a difficult password may be guessed. On the other hand, this reduces the time and resources required to execute such an attack.

A dictionary attack tried only those that are deemed most likely to succeed. Dictionary attacks often succeed because many people use short passwords or ordinary words.
